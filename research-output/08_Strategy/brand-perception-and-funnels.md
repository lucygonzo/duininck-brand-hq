## Part 2: The Leaky Funnel Audit

### Funnel 1: General Contractor / Public Works Decision Journey

```
STAGE 1: AWARENESS
"I need a heavy civil contractor for this project"
    |
    v
Google search: "heavy civil contractor Minnesota"
    |
    LEAK: Duininck not on page 1. GC clicks Ames, McCrossan, or Meyer.
    |
    v
STAGE 2: CONSIDERATION (if they already know Duininck)
GC checks duininck.com
    |
    LEAK: Homepage says "Building Strong Communities" (what does that mean?).
    No project portfolio with specs. No safety record. No case studies with outcomes.
    GC can't quickly verify "are they qualified for MY project?"
    |
    v
STAGE 3: EVALUATION
GC looks for proof: past projects similar to theirs
    |
    LEAK: 1 case study found. No filtering by project type, size, or location.
    Competitor sites have 50+ project pages with specs, photos, and outcomes.
    |
    v
STAGE 4: DECISION
GC sees Duininck on a bid list
    |
    (Here Duininck's reputation helps. They win on relationship + price + performance.)

```

**Where Duininck Loses GCs:**
1. Discovery (SEO failure)
2. Proof (no project portfolio with specs)
3. First impression (website doesn't communicate capability clearly)

**What Would Fix It:**
- 20+ project case study pages with specs, photos, timelines, outcomes
- Service pages optimized for search ("heavy civil contractor Minnesota," "road construction company MN")
- Safety record page with real statistics

## Part 3: Best-in-Class Family Company Benchmarks

### What Elite Family Companies Do That Duininck Doesn't (Yet)

**SC Johnson (5th generation, founded 1886):**
- Leads with "A Family Company" in every brand touchpoint. It's in the logo, the tagline, the URL slug.
- "What's Inside" transparency initiative turned family ownership into a consumer trust advantage.
- Connects generational ownership to long-term responsibility: "As a family company, we're always thinking about future generations."
- **Lesson for Duininck**: The family story isn't background context. It's the LEAD message. SC Johnson doesn't bury "5th generation" on the About page. It's the headline.

**JE Dunn Construction (100th anniversary, 2024):**
- "100 Stories" digital campaign: each story from a different employee, customer, or community member. Distributed across social, email, website.
- "Building a Legacy: A Century of Generosity" philanthropic campaign tied to the centennial.
- Commemorative logo on site signage, employee gear, vehicles.
- **Lesson for Duininck**: The centennial isn't a party. It's a 12-month brand campaign. JE Dunn started planning 2+ years early.

**Marlin Steel (family manufacturing, Baltimore):**
- Transformed from a commodity wire basket maker to a precision engineering company through brand and content strategy.
- CEO Drew Greenblatt became a manufacturing thought leader through LinkedIn, speaking, and press.
- Proves that a small family manufacturer can outcompete larger companies on brand.
- **Lesson for Duininck**: Nicole could be the voice. A CMO who left Fortune 200 for a roads-and-golf company in rural MN because she found "more purpose" is an inherently compelling story. She should be speaking and writing.

**Lincoln Electric (founded 1895, similar era to Duininck):**
- Legendary "guaranteed employment" policy since 1958. No layoffs in 65+ years.
- This single policy IS their employer brand. It doesn't need a marketing department to amplify it.
- **Lesson for Duininck**: What is Duininck's equivalent of "guaranteed employment"? The wellbeing framework could be it, but only if it's tangible and visible, not just a values statement.

**Edward Jones (founded 1922, similar era):**
- "Serving individual investors since 1922." Heritage is the first line.
- Small-town, relationship-first positioning in a Wall Street industry.
- Rural office model mirrors Duininck's rural operating base.
- **Lesson for Duininck**: "Building communities since 1926." The parallel is direct. Edward Jones proved that rural roots + heritage + personal service can compete with global scale.

### The Pattern: What All These Companies Have in Common

1. **Heritage is the HEADLINE, not a footnote.** SC Johnson, Edward Jones, Lincoln Electric all lead with their founding story. Duininck buries it.
2. **The family story creates trust.** Every benchmark company uses family ownership as a trust signal: "We're not going anywhere. We've been here for generations. We'll be here for yours."
3. **One signature commitment.** Lincoln Electric has guaranteed employment. SC Johnson has transparency. JE Dunn has generosity. Duininck's could be "whole person" wellbeing, but it needs to be a COMMITMENT, not a framework document.
4. **The CEO/leader IS the brand voice.** Drew Greenblatt at Marlin Steel, Fisk Johnson at SC Johnson. Nicole is perfectly positioned for this, but she's not publishing or speaking yet.
5. **Content is about PEOPLE, not capabilities.** JE Dunn's "100 Stories" works because every story is a person. Duininck's blog publishes twice a year with project updates. The people are missing.

## Part 4: The "Wow Nicole" Findings

These are the insights designed to make Nicole rethink assumptions:

### Finding 1: The Perception Score is 4.7 / 10
Duininck is a 9/10 company with a 4.7/10 brand. The gap between reality and perception is the single largest strategic problem. Everything else (content, SEO, social, recruiting) flows from closing this gap.

### Finding 2: Three Heritage Competitors Exist
Ulland Brothers (1920), Bolander (1924), and Duininck (1926) are ALL centenarians in MN heavy civil. "100 years" is necessary but not sufficient. "Four generations, 100 years, and the only company that builds roads, golf courses, and water systems" is the defensible position.

### Finding 3: Two Value Sets on Two Websites
The parent company and construction sites list completely different values. This needs resolution before unification can work externally. Is it corporate values + operating behaviors? Or has one set replaced the other? Nicole may not be aware this exists.

### Finding 4: The Best Brand Asset Is Invisible
Nicole's own quote ("I have more purpose here than I ever have, working for a company that builds roads and golf courses") is the most compelling piece of brand evidence in the entire research. A Fortune 200 CMO chose this. That endorsement belongs on the website, in recruiting materials, and in press coverage. Instead, it lives in a discovery call transcript.

### Finding 5: 700 Ambassadors Are Disconnected
Every competitor has the same labor shortage problem. None of them have 700 people already inside the company who could be sharing the story. Duininck's internal communication gap isn't just an HR problem. It's a missed marketing channel. If even 10% of employees shared one post per month, that's 70 authentic impressions in the exact communities where Duininck recruits.

### Finding 6: The Golf Division Has a Rolls-Royce in the Garage
Hazeltine National. Erin Hills (US Open venue). TPC courses. Donald Ross, Seth Raynor, Tillinghast restorations. This portfolio would be the centerpiece of any golf construction brand in the world. At Duininck, it's parked in a dark garage with the social media lights turned off and ~600 LinkedIn followers. Landscapes Unlimited claims "more Major Championship venues than any competitor." Duininck may be able to counter that claim, but nobody would know it.

### Finding 7: Construction Gets the Highest LinkedIn Engagement of Any Industry
4.0% engagement rate. Higher than tech. Higher than finance. Higher than healthcare. The audience is on LinkedIn, actively engaging with construction content. Duininck is posting to the platform with the most receptive audience in the world and barely showing up.

### Finding 8: The Centennial Clock Is Ticking
JE Dunn started planning 2+ years before their centennial. Duininck is inside 6 months. The July 25 event needs: multi-camera content capture crew, drone footage, social media war room, media outreach (60-90 days prior), commemorative branding on signage and gear, employee ambassador activation, "100 Stories" digital campaign, and press kits to ENR, Roads & Bridges, Golf Course Architecture, Minnesota Business Magazine, and the West Central Tribune. This is a once-in-100-years moment. There is no "we'll do it better next time."
