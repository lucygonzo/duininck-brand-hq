## Research Methodology
Audience research runs BEFORE competitive analysis. Understanding who we serve defines the playing field. Competitors are evaluated through the lens of "how well do they serve OUR audiences?" This research combines CMO-confirmed segments with public data from Indeed, Glassdoor, industry associations, and platform-specific behavior research.

## Segment 1: Prospective Employees (Seasonal + Full-Time)
**Priority**: CRITICAL (gates all recruiting content)
**Status**: Deep research complete

### Who They Are
Blue-collar operators, skilled trades, equipment operators, and laborers. Primarily seasonal hires needed every construction season (April-November). Concentrated in MN, SD, TX. Median age skews younger for seasonal roles. Many have CDLs or are willing to get one. They have smartphones but don't use LinkedIn.

### Where They Search for Jobs
| Platform | Usage | Notes |
|----------|-------|-------|
| Indeed | Primary | Duininck currently has ~25 listings in MN. $27-$35/hr for equipment operators. $3,000 sign-on bonus. |
| Facebook | High (passive) | Blue-collar workers scroll Facebook. They see job posts from friends sharing, not from searching. |
| Word of mouth | Very high | In rural MN/SD, people hear about jobs from church, grocery store, friends on other crews. |
| ZipRecruiter | Moderate | Aggregates listings. Duininck appears here. |
| Company website | Low intent | duininck.com/careers exists but is not where most discover the company. |
| LinkedIn | Very low | This audience does not use LinkedIn for job search. |

**Key insight**: 80% of construction employers reported difficulty filling positions. Hiring intent for blue-collar roles is up 10% even as other markets slow. Gen Z workers are choosing vocational training at higher rates (75% employment rate for vocational grads vs. 70% for college grads). The talent is out there but they're being recruited aggressively by every competitor.

### What Duininck's Job Postings Currently Say
- Pay range: $27-$35/hr (equipment operators)
- $3,000 sign-on bonus for heavy equipment operators
- Benefits: Strong health plans, dental & vision, company-paid life insurance
- Company description still references "third-generation" (needs update to fourth-generation)
- Does NOT mention the wellbeing framework
- Does NOT mention the centennial
- Does NOT mention "People. Values. Growth."
- Does NOT differentiate from any other construction company's posting

### What Competitors Say to This Audience

**Ames Construction** (primary talent competitor):
- 2,200 employees, $540M revenue
- Glassdoor: 3.8/5 stars, 78% recommend
- Indeed: 4.1/5 stars
- Pros: "good benefits and family friendly work culture," "safety and teamwork is main focus"
- Cons: "senior leadership," "work life balance," "nepotism runs rampant"
- 168 open jobs. Ranked #89 Best Companies to Work for in MN.

**Knife River** (primary talent competitor):
- 5,600 team members. Subsidiary of MDU Resources (public company).
- Glassdoor: 3.8/5 stars, 79% recommend
- Compensation/benefits: 3.8/5. Work-life balance: 3.7/5. Culture: 3.5/5.
- Pros: "great people," "safety oriented," "offers in-house paid CDL training"
- Cons: "long hours and travel," "poor management," "favoritism runs deep"
- Positions itself as "people-first" and "top 10 aggregate producer"
- 674 open positions on Glassdoor (massive recruiting operation)

**C.S. McCrossan**:
- 51-200 employees (much smaller). Founded 1956.
- Glassdoor: 4.0/5 stars (only 3 reviews, too small a sample)
- Pros: "hands-off management," "promotion is real possibility," "annual bonuses"
- Cons: "technologically behind the times," "very few young employees"
- Described as "unwelcoming union shop" by one reviewer

**Duininck** (current state):
- Glassdoor: 9 total reviews (extremely thin presence)
- Pros: "owners are great people that really care for their employees," "family oriented value," "opportunity to grow," "low turnover rate"
- Cons: "long hours," "work canceled due to weather," "travel and lodging"
- No star rating prominently visible due to low review count

### Where Duininck Loses This Audience
1. **Discovery**: Job postings read like every other construction company. No mention of the things that make Duininck different (100 years, family, wellbeing framework, values).
2. **The Glassdoor check**: Only 9 reviews. When a recruit's spouse googles "Duininck reviews," they see almost nothing. Ames has 114 reviews, Knife River has 141. Thin review count signals "small" even though Duininck has 700+ employees.
3. **The "so what?" test**: $27-$35/hr is competitive but not dominant. Benefits are standard. Nothing in the posting makes a 25-year-old choose Duininck over Knife River's 5,600-person operation with paid CDL training.
4. **Scale perception**: Knife River has 674 open positions. Duininck has 25. Even though Duininck may be a better place to work, the posting volume signals that Knife River is the "real" opportunity.

### Where Duininck Could Win This Audience
1. **The "whole person" positioning**: No competitor uses this language. Not Ames ("family friendly"), not Knife River ("people-first"), not McCrossan. The 6-dimension wellbeing framework (Career, Family/Social, Finances, Health, Community, Spiritual) is genuinely unique. It needs to show up in every job posting.
2. **The 100-year proof**: "Join a company that's been building for 100 years and treats you like a whole person." No competitor can claim this.
3. **The spouse test**: Blue-collar hiring decisions are often household decisions. The wellbeing framework speaks to spouses: "They care about your family, your finances, your health." This is the angle no competitor plays.
4. **Glassdoor activation**: Employees already say positive things ("owners are great people," "low turnover"). A deliberate campaign to encourage reviews would change the perception overnight. Target: 50+ reviews within 6 months.
5. **Centennial as recruiting anchor**: "Come build the next 100 years with us" is more compelling than any pay bump.

## Segment 2: Public Works & General Contractors
**Priority**: PRIMARY (bread-and-butter revenue audience)
**Status**: Deep research complete

### Who They Are
County road department managers, municipal engineers, state DOT project managers (especially MnDOT), and general contractors who bid and contract heavy civil work. Decision-makers who evaluate contractors through formal bid processes.

### How They Find Contractors

**Formal bid process (government/public works)**:
- MnDOT uses a formal bid letting process. Projects posted on dot.state.mn.us/bidlet/
- Contractors must register with MN Secretary of State and provide 5% proposal guarantee
- Contracts awarded to lowest responsible and responsive bidder
- Reputation, safety record, and past performance matter for "responsible" determination
- Bid results are public (dot.state.mn.us/bidlet/abstract.html), meaning competitors can see every win/loss

**Private sector (general contractors)**:
- Relationship-driven. GCs work with contractors they know and trust.
- Prequalification lists matter. Being on the right lists means getting invited to bid.
- Trade associations (AGC, NAPA) create networking and visibility.
- Project references and case studies influence shortlisting.

### What Moves This Audience
| Factor | Weight | Duininck's Position |
|--------|--------|-------------------|
| Price (lowest responsible bid) | High | Competitive but not unique |
| Safety record | High | Strong but not prominently showcased |
| Past project experience | High | 100 years of projects, poorly documented online |
| Equipment and capacity | Medium | Full fleet, vertically integrated |
| Reputation/references | High | Excellent locally, invisible digitally |
| Bonding capacity | Medium | Needs verification |
| Minority/DBE status | Situational | N/A |

### Where This Audience Congregates
- MnDOT bidlet website (for project announcements)
- AGC (Associated General Contractors) of Minnesota events
- NAPA (National Asphalt Pavement Association) conferences
- County engineer associations
- ENR (Engineering News-Record) regional rankings
- Trade shows: World of Asphalt, ConExpo
- Local Rotary/Chamber events (in smaller markets)

### Key Finding
This audience doesn't "choose" Duininck from a website. They encounter Duininck through bid lists, past project experience, and industry reputation. The brand work for this audience is about REINFORCEMENT, not discovery. When a county engineer sees the Duininck name on a bid, we want them to think "100 years, solid reputation, safe crews, they always deliver." That association is built through:
1. Consistent trade publication presence
2. Award submissions (ENR rankings, NAPA awards)
3. Case study documentation (before/after, on-time/on-budget)
4. Safety record marketing
5. Community reputation in operating markets

## Segment 3: Golf Course Architects & Owners
**Priority**: PRIMARY (Niche)
**Status**: Deep research complete

### Who They Are
Course architects (members of ASGCA), private club boards, resort operators, and municipal golf authorities. A small, tight-knit, prestige-driven community where reputation travels fast and portfolio quality is everything.

### How They Find Builders
| Channel | Details |
|---------|---------|
| ASGCA (American Society of Golf Course Architects) | The primary professional association. ASGCA maintains an Industry Providers database. Many members prefer GCBAA builders. |
| GCBAA (Golf Course Builders Association of America) | The trade association for golf builders. Membership signals legitimacy. Founded early 1970s. |
| Architect relationships | "Having experience with similar projects and a relationship with the architect on past projects is always preferred." Direct relationships drive most builder selection. |
| Golf Course Architecture magazine | The industry publication. Features project profiles that influence architect perception. |
| Instagram | Architects and club boards use Instagram to evaluate project quality. Visual portfolio is essential. |
| Conference networking | ASGCA annual meetings, Golf Industry Show, GCBAA events |

### The Competitive Landscape for This Audience
**Landscapes Unlimited** is the dominant competitor:
- Founded 1976. Based in Lincoln, NE.
- Self-described as "the recognized leader in golf course development, construction, and renovation"
- Claims "more Major Championship venues and top-100 courses than any competitor"
- 2,500+ completed projects
- Part of a family of companies including Landscapes Golf Management
- Active on LinkedIn with project content
- ASGCA Industry Provider

**Wadsworth Golf**: Strong portfolio, national presence
**Heritage Links**: Specialized in high-end restorations

### Duininck Golf's Untapped Advantage
Duininck Golf has restored courses by Donald Ross, Seth Raynor, and Tillinghast. They've worked on Hazeltine National, Erin Hills, and TPC venues. This is WORLD-CLASS pedigree that is nearly invisible:
- ~600 LinkedIn followers (Landscapes Unlimited has dramatically more visibility)
- Social accounts went dark (Sam and a few guys ran them with an agency, stopped)
- Portfolio buried on duininckgolf.com without the prestige framing it deserves
- No connection shown to the 100-year parent company heritage

### What Moves This Audience
1. **Portfolio quality**: "Show me the courses you've built." Before/after photos of restorations. The names of the original architects matter enormously (Ross, Raynor, Tillinghast = instant credibility).
2. **Architect relationships**: Repeat work with respected architects signals trust.
3. **Craft attention**: This audience cares about drainage, shaping, grow-in, turf quality. The technical craft matters as much as the visual result.
4. **The "one year later" story**: Golf courses need 12+ months after construction to reach beauty. The time-lapse story (construction > grow-in > maturity) is the most compelling content type for this audience.
5. **Heritage**: Being part of a 100-year family company that also builds highways and water systems is a differentiator. It signals financial stability, equipment access, and operational depth that pure golf builders can't match.

### Where Duininck Loses This Audience
1. Dark social accounts mean zero ongoing visibility with architects
2. Portfolio not presented with the prestige framing it deserves
3. No active content showing current projects or grow-in progress
4. GCBAA membership status unclear (this matters for architect selection)
5. No presence in Golf Course Architecture magazine recently

### Where Duininck Could Win
1. Restart Instagram with a portfolio-first approach (Ross/Raynor/Tillinghast restorations front and center)
2. Frame the parent company connection: "Backed by a century of building infrastructure"
3. The "one year later" content series: show a completed course at 0 months, 6 months, 12 months
4. GCBAA visibility and ASGCA conference presence
5. Golf Course Architecture magazine feature/advertising

## Segment 4: Current Employees (700+ Field Workers)
**Priority**: CRITICAL (Internal)
**Status**: Research complete

### The Communication Challenge
- 700+ employees, ~90% in the field
- No corporate email for most field workers
- No intranet
- No internal comms platform
- 83% of frontline workers globally don't have a corporate email address
- 45% don't have access to company intranet at work
- Current communication happens through managers and job site huddles

### How Other Companies Solve This
| Approach | Examples | Pros | Cons |
|----------|----------|------|------|
| Mobile app (employee comms) | Beekeeper, Staffbase, LumApps | Push notifications reach phones. Photo/video sharing. Surveys. | Requires app download. Adoption can be low. |
| SMS-based | Team Engine, Heymarket | Reaches everyone. No app needed. | Limited to text. Not visual. |
| WhatsApp groups | Common in smaller companies | Free. Already on phones. | Hard to manage at 700+ scale. No analytics. |
| Digital signage at break areas | Common in manufacturing | Reaches people passively. | Only works at fixed locations, not scattered job sites. |
| Printed materials (toolbox talks) | Traditional approach | Familiar format. | One-way, no engagement tracking. |

### What Content Would Reach This Audience
- Centennial pride content: "You're part of something 100 years in the making"
- Safety recognition: "Crew X completed 365 days incident-free"
- Project spotlights: "Here's what YOUR work looks like from a drone"
- Benefits reminders: Wellness programs, tuition reimbursement, 401k match
- Seasonal kickoff/wrap: Mark the start and end of construction season
- Family events: Content their spouses and kids see too

### Key Finding
The 700+ field employees are the most undertapped brand ambassadors in the company. If each employee shared one piece of content per year with their personal network, that's 700+ authentic impressions in the communities where Duininck recruits. The internal comms strategy isn't just about informing employees. It's about turning them into a volunteer marketing army, but only if they feel genuinely connected to the story.

## Segment 7: Subcontractors & Trade Partners
**Priority**: HIGH (directly affects project quality and capacity)
**Status**: Research complete

### Who They Are
Specialty contractors and suppliers that Duininck partners with on projects: electrical, environmental remediation, structural, geotechnical, landscaping, and specialty paving. In Minnesota, this is a defined ecosystem. The Minnesota Subcontractors Association (MSA) represents 200+ firms and 1,000+ individual members across every construction specialty trade.

### Why They Matter
- Poor subcontractor management contributes to over 30% of project delays and cost overruns
- The best subs can CHOOSE who they work with. A strong brand attracts better partners.
- Under Minnesota law, subcontractors without written contracts are considered employees. Duininck's legal and operational rigor matters.
- Subs talk. Their experience on Duininck projects shapes word-of-mouth reputation in the contractor community.
- Repeat sub relationships drive project quality and reduce onboarding cost

### How They Evaluate Prime Contractors
| Factor | What They Look For |
|--------|-------------------|
| Payment reliability | Do they pay on time? (This is #1 for subs. A reputation for slow payment kills partnerships.) |
| Safety culture | Will my crew be safe on their jobsite? |
| Project management | Are they organized? Clear scopes? Fair change order process? |
| Repeat work | Can I build a book of business with this prime? |
| Reputation | What do other subs say about working with them? |
| Scale and pipeline | Do they have enough work to keep me busy? |

### Where They Congregate
- Minnesota Subcontractors Association (msamn.com) events and networking
- AGC of Minnesota contractor mixers and events
- Plan rooms and bid listing services
- Jobsite relationships (foremen and project managers build sub networks)
- Word of mouth (the contractor community in MN is small and tight)

### The Duininck Opportunity
Duininck's values ("Servant Leadership," "Integrity") translate directly to subcontractor relationships. A prime contractor that pays on time, runs safe sites, and treats subs with respect is genuinely rare. If Duininck already does this, the sub community should know it. If they don't talk about it externally, it's invisible goodwill.

### Recommended Actions
1. Document subcontractor satisfaction (even informal pulse surveys)
2. Feature sub partnerships in project case studies: "We partnered with [sub] on this project"
3. Attend MSA events with brand presence (sponsor, speak, network)
4. Consider a "preferred subcontractor" program that subs can reference
5. Ask Nicole: how does Duininck select and manage subs today? Any pain points?

## Segment 8: Civil Engineers & Design Consultants
**Priority**: HIGH (influence project shortlists and specifications)
**Status**: Research complete

### Who They Are
Professional/technical consultants who design projects, write specifications, and manage construction on behalf of public agencies. In Minnesota, MnDOT maintains a formal pre-qualification system for consulting firms. These engineers don't select contractors directly (that's the bid process), but they:
- Write specifications that favor certain contractor capabilities
- Recommend contractors for negotiated work
- Serve as construction managers who evaluate contractor performance
- Build relationships that lead to future project opportunities

### How the MnDOT Consultant System Works
- MnDOT maintains a pre-qualification program for Professional/Technical consultants
- Each project has an "Official List" identifying eligible firms
- Firms must be pre-qualified in specific "work types" to submit for projects
- MnDOT publishes upcoming P/T projects regularly (dot.state.mn.us/consult/notices.html)
- Key contact: MnDOT Liaison to P/T Consultants, Laurie Ryan (651-245-8368)

### Why They Matter for Duininck
- On negotiated or design-build projects, engineer recommendations directly influence contractor selection
- Engineers who've had positive experiences with Duininck will write specs that align with Duininck's capabilities
- County engineers (there's one for each of MN's 87 counties) influence local project decisions
- The engineer community is small. Reputation spreads fast, both positive and negative.

### Where They Congregate
- MnDOT consultant networking events
- American Society of Civil Engineers (ASCE) Minnesota chapter
- American Council of Engineering Companies (ACEC) Minnesota
- County Engineers Association of Minnesota
- Local plan review meetings and pre-bid conferences

### The Duininck Opportunity
Engineers care about competence, reliability, and collaboration. They want contractors who solve problems instead of creating them, who build what's drawn instead of cutting corners, and who communicate proactively when issues arise. Duininck's 100-year track record IS the proof point for this audience. A well-documented project portfolio (specs met, on time, on budget, no claims) is the most powerful marketing asset for engineers.

### Recommended Actions
1. Build a project portfolio page organized by project TYPE (not just location) so engineers can find relevant experience
2. Include technical specs in case studies: tonnage, materials, methods, timeline adherence
3. Track and publicize performance metrics: on-time completion rate, safety incident rate, change order percentage
4. Attend ACEC/ASCE Minnesota events. Sponsor continuing education sessions.
5. Ask Nicole: does Duininck have a county engineer contact list? Which county relationships are strongest?

## Trade School & Vocational Pipeline (Recruiting Sub-Segment)
**Priority**: HIGH (feeds the annual seasonal hiring need)
**Status**: Research complete

### The Pipeline Opportunity
Duininck needs seasonal workers EVERY year. Instead of competing for experienced operators on Indeed, what if the pipeline was already built? Minnesota has a robust system of construction-focused vocational programs right in Duininck's backyard:

### Programs Within Duininck's Recruiting Radius

**Ridgewater College (Willmar, MN)** [Duininck's backyard]
- Construction Trades program with multiple pathways
- Heavy equipment classes: forklift, skid loader, mobile crane certification, CDL prep
- Basic Construction Skills: OSHA 10-hour safety card, hand/power tools, construction math
- Construction Craft Laborer: exposure to carpentry, masonry, ironworking, electrical, welding, heavy equipment
- Electrician program
- Customized Training Solutions for employers (Duininck could sponsor a cohort)

**Central Lakes College (Brainerd/Staples, MN)**
- Heavy Equipment Operation & Maintenance program (1-year and 2-year tracks)
- Students earn: OSHA 10-hour card, Class A CDL, CPR/AED cert, MN aggregate production cert
- Hands-on learning with excavators, dozers, skid steers
- Top employers: heavy equipment dealers and earth-moving contractors

**Dakota County Technical College (Rosemount, MN)**
- Heavy Construction Equipment Technology program
- Year-round program with dealer and contractor partnerships

**Minnesota State Commercial Driver Academy**
- Standardized CDL training across three campuses
- Designed for employer partnerships

**MnDOT Youth Pipeline**
- Operating Engineers Local 49 Pathway for high school students through MN Virtual Academy
- High school students learn heavy equipment operation while earning credit
- Direct career pathway into construction

### What Competitors Are Doing With Pipelines
Research needed: Are Ames, Knife River, or McCrossan sponsoring cohorts, offering apprenticeships, or partnering with these schools? If not, this is first-mover territory for Duininck.

### Recommended Actions
1. **Ridgewater College partnership**: Sponsor a "Duininck Scholars" cohort in Construction Craft Laborer program. Tuition assistance in exchange for seasonal commitment. This is literally in Willmar.
2. **Central Lakes College pipeline**: Attend career fairs. Offer site visits for heavy equipment students. "Come see what a real jobsite looks like."
3. **Apprenticeship program**: Formalize seasonal-to-permanent pathways. "Start in summer, stay for a career."
4. **High school outreach**: Connect with the Local 49/MnDOT youth pathway. Brand awareness starts before they're job seekers.
5. **Centennial recruiting angle**: "Come build the next 100 years with us" as a campaign targeting graduating students.
6. **CDL sponsorship**: Pay for CDL training in exchange for a seasonal commitment. Knife River already offers in-house paid CDL training. Duininck needs to match or beat this.
